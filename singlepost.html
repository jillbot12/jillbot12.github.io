Castaways, Ethnotourism, the Game of Apocalypse

AUTHOR: Henry Peterson
February 23rd, 2016

If you’ve ever wanted to languish on a desert island, your day may have finally arrived. For a modest fee, the travel company Docastaway will strand you on one of a number of designated, isolated islands. If you’re so inclined, some even come with a tiki bar. What’s the appeal? Unlike high-adrenaline tourism, Docastaway is not fed on fantasies of adventure, but of apocalypse. If adventure is threatened, tenuous life congealed into story, then the castaway has no stories to tell and no adventure to recount. There is no one there to listen.

A slightly different twist is the kind of survival/ethno-tourism the company Adastra Adventures provides. For $3000 (airfare not included) you can “rekindle the knowledge mankind is losing due to our modern day lives” through basic wilderness training in the proximity of “indigenous people” (read Primitive). Their familiar sales pitch invites us to “revert” to an Edenic state of pre-history; a static, homogenous time that comfortingly never changes. But on a desert island our castaway falls completely outside of history and socially structured time. Labels like Primitive or Modern are socio-cultural designations—they do not exist in a vacuum. The castaway is alone—a horizon unto themselves—an apocalypse of one. As their past within society recedes, so too does their future.

It is helpful to remember the El Salvadorian fisherman and very real castaway who was rescued in the Marshal Islands after a 438 day drift across the Pacific. Alone on a small fishing boat, José Salvador Alvarenga survived on little more than gull blood, raw turtle meat, and the occasional, floating scrap of garbage. Every shipwrecked day would start with a brisk walk—“I would stroll back and forth on the boat and imagine that I was wandering the world. By doing this I could make myself believe that I was actually doing something. Not just sitting there, thinking about dying.”
